Host, pathogen, and microbiome factors associated with Buruli ulcer disease severity

Buruli Ulcer is a neglected tropical disease caused by the flesh-eating bacterium Mycobacterium ulcerans resulting in subcutaneous necrotic skin infections. If not treated promptly, the disease could result in prolonged morbidity including disfiguring lesions requiring corrective surgery. According to the World Health Organisation, 33 countries have reported the disease, with 98% of reported cases corresponding to the African region. In Australia, outbreaks predominantly occur in Victoria. The exponential growth of Buruli Ulcer cases since 2004 and its spread into the Mornington and Bellarine Peninsulas, Geelong and inner Melbourne suburbs has called for an urgent scientific response. The reasons for the epidemic are unclear but may relate to genotypic changes in M. ulcerans that have made it more pathogenic or transmissible. Furthermore, there is currently no information available on the relationship between the genotype of an isolate or that of the patient and the clinical severity of the disease. In addition, there have been numerous reports of secondary infections by opportunistic pathogens which makes treatment complicated. Using samples collected from an observational cohort of Buruli ulcer patients from the Bellarine Peninsula, this project aims to conduct a range of genomic analyses to understand the determinants of Buruli ulcer severity. Specifically, this project will analyse genetic variations of the pathogen, human host, and microbiome content to explore whether these may influence ulcer severity. Preliminary sequencing of M. ulcerans genomes from a limited number of ulcer swabs (n=50) has been conducted using RNA probe-based capture and Illumina sequencing. The results of the analysis of these samples will be presented, along with future directions of the research project to achieve the aims mentioned above.
