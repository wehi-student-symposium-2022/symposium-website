Small-molecule modulation of BAK- and BAX-mediated mitochondrial apoptosis

Mitochondrial or intrinsic apoptosis is a regulated cell death modality that is crucial for not only normal embryonic development, but also tissue homeostasis of multi-cellular organisms. B-cell lymphoma 2 (BCL-2) family proteins play an important role in regulating the mitochondrial apoptosis pathway. BCL-2 family members are grouped into 3 categories. One category comprises anti-apoptotic proteins, which inhibit apoptosis (BCL-2, BCL-XL, BCL-W, MCL-1 and BFL-1/A1) while another consists of the effector proteins BAX and BAK, which promote apoptosis. BH3-only proteins, the third category of BCL-2 proteins, promote apoptosis by neutralising the first category of anti-apoptotic members or activating the second category of effector proteins BAX and BAK.

Dysfunction of mitochondrial apoptosis is involved in many human pathologies. For example, down-regulation of mitochondrial apoptosis is implicated in certain types of cancer, while excessive activation of this pathway is observed in many degenerative diseases. Anti-apoptotic BCL-2 proteins have been targeted by compounds to treat certain types of cancer. On the other hand, it remains unclear whether small molecules can directly target effector proteins BAK and BAX to modulate cell death.

Here we describe a series of compounds, series 7, that can inhibit BAX-mediated apoptosis but potentiate BAK-mediated apoptosis. Using cellular and biochemical assays, we discovered that the compounds inhibit BAX- but sensitizes BAK-mediated mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization. Mechanistically, the compounds inhibit early steps of BAX activation, which is the VDAC2-mediated BAX recruitment from cytosol to mitochondria. However, the compounds can also potentiate activation of BAK, although the mechanism is still unclear. These results not only pave the way for the development of therapeutics against certain diseases, but also help to reveal new biological pathways regulating apoptosis.
