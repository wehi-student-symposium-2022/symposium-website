Characterisation of PROTAC-induced branched ubiquitination for targeted protein degradation

Ubiquitination is a critical component of post-translational modifications that regulate cellular protein functions and turnover. The sequential cascade is mediated by the ubiquitin-activating enzyme, ubiquitin-conjugating enzymes and E3 ubiquitin ligases (E3s) to conjugate the small 8.6 kDa protein, ubiquitin, to a lysine residue on a substrate protein. The attachment of ubiquitin to an internal lysine of another ubiquitin molecule produces polyubiquitin chains, with 8 possible homotypic linkage types that can be generated to produce distinct cellular signals. For example, K48 linkages dictate ubiquitinated substrates to the proteasome for degradation. Alternatively, ubiquitin branching occurs when a single ubiquitin molecule is simultaneously modified on two acceptor lysine residues. The complex architecture of ubiquitin branches greatly expands the complexity of cellular signals and expands the ubiquitin code. For example, K29/K48 or K11/K48 branded ubiquitin chains have been found to facilitate protein turnover in cells.

Proteolysis targeting chimera (PROTAC) technology utilises hetero-bifunctional compounds to hijack E3s for targeted protein degradation of neo-substrates. Recently, K29/48-branched ubiquitin chains catalysed by TRIP12 in coordination with cullin complexes has been shown to accelerate PROTAC-induced degradation of BRD4. Interestingly, the E3 ligase UBR4 can function redundantly with TRIP12 and is capable of catalysing K11/K48-branched ubiquitin chains on endogenous substrates. Thus, I hypothesis that the branching structure of ubiquitin chains promotes PROTAC-mediated neo-substrate degradation, which is independent of linkage type.

Here I present an optimised approach for the detection of ubiquitin branching on a model substrate containing HaloTag, EGFP NanoLuc and FKBPF36V, using Lbpro-mediated ubiquitin-clipping. Further investigation into the architecture and function of PROTAC-induced ubiquitin branches and characterisation of branching events in different cellular and disease contexts will greatly expand our understanding of the cellular ubiquitin code and guide the rational design of more efficient PROTAC compounds.
