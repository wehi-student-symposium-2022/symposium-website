Understanding the role of mitochondria and ubiquitin signalling defects in early onset Parkinson’s disease

Background:

Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disorder and is pathologically characterised by dopaminergic neuronal loss in the substantia nigra and intracellular aggregates of a-synuclein called Lewy bodies. 90% of cases are late-onset and idiopathic with no clear genetic basis and up to 10% of cases are genetic and generally early onset (less than age 50). Although the causes of the dopaminergic neuron death that drives PD remain unclear, defects in mitochondria are implicated and two key genes linked with early onset Parkinson’s disease (EOPD), Parkin and PINK1, are critical for efficient turnover of damaged or dysfunctional mitochondria via selective autophagy (mitophagy). PD is also characterised by defects in ubiquitin-dependent processes. These are wide ranging and include: proteosomal degradation, autophagy, mitochondrial homeostasis, membrane trafficking and neuroinflammation. Furthermore, one of the biggest risk factors for developing PD is ageing and tissue resident senescent cells are implicated as key drivers of disorders of ageing including PD.

Aims/methods:

Establish a pipeline for EOPD skin punch biopsy and peripheral blood collection

Characterise mitochondrial form and function in EOPD patient-derived dermal fibroblasts and in iNPC-derived dopaminergic neurons.

Characterise ubiquitin signalling in EOPD patient-derived cells in comparison with cells derived from control subjects

Determine contribution of mitochondrial form and function towards senescence induction in EOPD patient-derived cells in comparison with cells derived from control subjects

Results: Currently have obtained 26 (21 EOPD + 5 controls) skin punch biopsies and associated peripheral blood samples.

Significance: Further understanding the molecular mechanisms and heterogeneity of EOPD based on underlying genetic vulnerabilities can lead to improved biomarkers and inform targeted precision medicine approaches in the future.
