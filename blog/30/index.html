Single Cell Scoring of Molecular Phenotypes

Molecular biology indicates that, just like fingerprints, every individual has a unique DNA sequence, and each cell of the individual has identical genetic material. However, the different molecular phenotypes in our body arise due to variable gene expression. Depending on their spatial and temporal states, different cells express distinct genes or groups of genes (gene sets) and supress others to achieve the desired function. We can now quantify the gene expression of a single cell, that can be used to identify its phenotype and thereby define the cellâ€™s state. Most existing computational cell phenotyping approaches for single cells focus on discerning cell type identity alone and thereby often ignore finer phenotypes.

Numerous computational methods have been developed to infer cell identity and a few methods have been developed to assess molecular phenotypes. Lack of extensive benchmarking has made it difficult to apply these methods to infer useful biology. Here, I develop a computational benchmarking study wherein I use cell type identification as a higher order molecular phenotyping task to evaluate different methods. As part of the benchmark, I evaluate four cellular phenotyping methods across five scRNA-seq datasets spanning five sequencing technologies. The benchmarks assess the ability of these methods to identify cell types, including rare cell types. I show that a simple rank-based method that is computationally efficient has similar performance to complex methods and is therefore suitable for studies atlas-scale data. I then use the best performing method to develop a novel analytical approach that profiles cellular phenotypes in cancer across the hallmark spectrum. The results from my study will enable dissection of molecular phenotypes in cancer systems at a cellular level and will allow the identification of rare cell types, such as those that are unresponsive to therapies.
