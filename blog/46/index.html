Evaluating the effect of transmembrane domain oligomerisation on the safety and efficacy of CAR T cell therapies

Chimeric Antigen Receptor (CAR) T cell therapy is a new treatment for cancer patients with six CAR T cell products having been approved by the FDA since late 2017. CAR T cells are produced by modifying patient T cells with engineered receptors (CARs) that bind specifically to target cancer antigens. Once the extracellular antigen-binding domain of a CAR binds to a cancer antigen, the receptors activate T cell effector functions, including secretion of granzymes and perforin that induce apoptosis of surrounding tumour cells. Despite the success of CAR T cell therapy in treating B cell malignancies, the frequent complication of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) prevents it from becoming a standard treatment for cancer.

 

To address this issue, different CAR domains have been modified to improve their safety and efficacy. However, the mechanism of how these structural changes affect CAR T cell function remains largely unknown. This research builds upon the Call lab’s novel finding that programmable membrane proteins (proMPs) – synthetic transmembrane domains (TMDs) computationally designed and experimentally validated to form monomers, dimers, trimers and tetramers - can be used to scale CAR T cell potency. As a correlation between CAR T cell function and oligomeric state was observed in primary mouse-derived HER2-targetting CAR T cells, this project will first test whether the correlation holds true for primary human-derived HER2-targetting CAR T cells. The proMPs will then be integrated into the CD19-targeting Yescarta CAR product to test whether safety can be improved whilst maintaining tumour killing efficacy compared to a clinical reference. Should this be the case, our ultimate goal is to progress to clinical trials to show that we can improve CAR T cell therapy, while reducing CRS burden in patients.
