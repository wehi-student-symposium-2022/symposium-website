Applying single-cell multi-omics approach to discover changes in circulating immune cells under long-term venetoclax treatment

Venetoclax (VEN) is a BH3-mimetic drug that selectively blocks the pro-survival protein BCL2, thereby initiating cell killing in cells that are highly reliant on this pro-survival protein. Venetoclax shows remarkable clinical efficiency in treating certain blood cancers, such as chronic lymphocytic leukemia and acute myeloid leukemia. However, most patients will develop relapsed disease while on VEN and further treatment options are then limited. My research focuses on discovering novel approaches to enhance the efficacy of VEN, such as combining VEN with immunotherapy. Therefore, it is crucial to understand how long-term VEN treatment impacts the circulating immune cells in patients. We applied single-cell multi-omics method (CITE-seq) to peripheral blood samples from 5 patients with solid cancer before and after long-term VEN treatment. CITE-seq allows us to profile the transcriptome and surface proteins of the same cell simultaneously. Data were integrated with harmony for batch correction and clustered based on gene expression. Pseudo-bulk analysis was then applied within cell subsets to identify consistent changes among the five patients. We found significant depletion and transcriptome changes of B cells with low expression of MCL1 after VEN treatment. By contrast, we observed few changes in cell composition, gene expression and key checkpoint molecules in T/NK-cell subsets after VEN treatment. Our data shows that VEN therapy has minimal impact on circulating T/NK cells, suggesting the potential of combining VEN with cancer immunotherapies.
