Determining the impact of antigen dose and affinity on B cell antibody isotype switching

Background: Once activated, B cells can switch the type of antibody they produce through a process known as class switch recombination (CSR), allowing for more effective antibody responses tailored to the type of infection encountered. This process requires expression of both activation-induced cytidine deaminase (AID) and germline RNA transcripts (GLTs). Previous work by Horton et al established a model capable of accurately describing rates of CSR in cells stimulated with cytokine, based on patterns of AID and GLT expression. This study sought to test the generalisability of this model in cells stimulated by antigen via the B cell receptor (BCR).

Methods: This study primarily involved the use of transgenic mice with B cells expressing the same BCR specific to hen egg lysozyme (HEL). B cells were harvested from spleens and cultured in varying concentrations of HEL, along with lower affinity variants of the antigen. Analysis was conducted via flow cytometry to determine surface Ig expression, along with single cell RT-qPCR to quantify RNA expression of GLTs responsible for driving CSR.

Results: CSR to IgG1 induced by antigen stimulation required a higher concentration of antigen when affinity was decreased. Importantly, the proportion of IgG1+ cells induced by lower affinity HEL was lower than that of native HEL, with higher concentrations unable to ‘rescue’ this process. This was regulated by the proportion of cells that express the Iy1 GLT, but not the level of expression itself, which is consistent among any cells expressing the transcript.

Conclusions: These results demonstrate that antigen stimulation regulates CSR via altering the probability a cell expresses the Iy1 GLT. Importantly, it was found that the response to lower affinity antigen is subdued and cannot be recovered by increased concentration, indicating that affinity sensing via the BCR is a mechanism by which B cells can regulate switching fates.
