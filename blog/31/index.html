High fat diets rapidly alter gut homeostasis

Westernized diet, featured by higher intake of saturated fats and lower consumption of fibres, represents a chief environmental factor of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). In mice, prolonged high fat diets (HFD) induce low-grade chronic intestinal inflammation characterized by increased pro-inflammatory cytokines in the intestine and lead to increased intestinal permeability. Although the symptoms and inflammation induced by long-term HFD during obesity and diabetes have been well characterized, the early functional changes that occur in response to diet changes that precede the establishment of chronic intestinal inflammation remain unclear.

Using multi-parameter flow cytometry and single cell transcriptomics, we have analysed the gut immune landscape after 2, 7 and 21 days under HFD. Our study revealed that innate lymphoid cells (ILC) activity was altered after 2 days on HFD followed by a dysregulation of gd T cells and regulatory T cells after 7 and 21 days. HFD induced a rapid loss of IL-22 by ILC3 and an increase of IL-5 by ILC2. These data suggest that change in diet composition is rapidly sensed by ILCs which induces a shift from type 3 immune response which is predominant in gut during homeostasis towards type 2 immunity.â€¯

HFD are enriched with saturated long-chain fatty acids (LCFA) which are associated with negative health impacts including obesity, diabetes and IBD. To evaluate the regulatory role of LCFA, we analysed the cytokine production of purified ILCs in presence of saturated and unsaturated LCFA. The presence of LCFA inhibited both constitutive and IL-23 induced secretion of IL-22 by ILC3.

Overall, our study demonstrates that HFD induce rapid modification of immune response in the gut mucosa and the changes in fatty acid metabolism in ILC alter their functions.
