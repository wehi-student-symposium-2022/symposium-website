Molecular signatures of drug sensitivity in ovarian carcinoma cell lines

Ovarian carcinoma has the 6th highest mortality rate of any cancer in women. The poor prognosis can be largely attributed to the lack of targeted treatment. Standard treatment for all patients, is debulking surgery followed by carboplatin and paclitaxel chemotherapy. While initial responses to this regime are hopeful, 75% of patients will experience recurrence within 3 years, and only 20% will become long term survivors. Treatments targeted against patient heterogeneity are crucial to improving outcomes, however developing new therapeutics is a costly, time-consuming, risky process. Drug repurposing is an emerging drug discovery strategy that overcomes many of these obstacles. It explores the therapeutic utility of clinically approved compounds for off-patent use, defined by specific molecular targets. Given the potential for rapid, cost-effective translation of repurposed drugs, large, publicly available databases of drug response in cancer cell lines and other patient-derived disease models have been established. This project is utilising these databases, including the Genomic of Drug Sensitivity in Cancer and the Cancer Cell Line Encyclopaedia to interrogate drug responses in 24 ovarian carcinoma cell lines. Statistical methods to integrate IC50 values with the AUC of dose response, will be used to identify exceptional cellular responses to different drugs targeting common signalling pathways. These cell line-specific vulnerabilities will be confirmed through gene expression and copy number analysis, and where drug sensitivities are supported by gene sets, molecular signatures of treatment efficacy will be built. Future work will explore an ovarian carcinoma patient cohort to distinguish patient subsets that are best represented by these cell lines. Targeted therapies for patients will then be inferred from the developed molecular signatures of drug sensitivity. Utilising drug response data in cell lines that are representative off patient disease is a crucial way rapidly improving the treatment landscape of ovarian carcinoma.
