Variations in antigen binding domains of logic gating circuits has significant impact on functionality

Glioblastoma is an aggressive and malignant brain tumour and five-year survival rates are estimated at only 5%. Consequently, new, targeted treatments are required to enhance patient survival. Chimeric Antigen Receptor (CAR) T cells have revolutionized the treatment of many types of haematological malignancies; providing a patient’s immune cells with a cancer target-specific synthetic receptor which mediates recognition and elimination of malignant cells.

The Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor (EGFR) variant III (EGFRvIII) is a tumour-specific mutation occurring in a subset of glioblastoma tumours. We previously published the characterisation of a novel EGFRvIII-specific high affinity CAR (GCT02), in primary murine T cells. We now extend this work, demonstrating the function of GCT02 in primary human T cells.  

GCT02 CAR T cells exhibited specific, comparable cytotoxicity and favourable cytokine profile compared to the clinical benchmark EGFRvIII-specific 2173 scFv CAR. Furthermore, a single intravenous infusion of the GCT02 CAR T cells was sufficient to mediate elimination of two orthotopic models of glioblastoma. We have also demonstrated that GCT02 CAR T cells have a favourable safety profile as they are unresponsive to primary EGFR+ keratinocytes.

To enhance GCT02 CAR clinical efficacy we incorporated it into the published Synthetic Notch logic gating system as an ON gate. However, we found that despite high expression of either GCT02-SynNotch CAR or 2173-SynNotch CAR no downstream induction was observed. These findings suggest that there are limitations to the deployment of this technology that require further elucidation.

 

In summary, we have developed a high affinity safe EGFRvIII specific CAR and shown that it functions in primary human T cells in vitro and in vivo in two orthotopic models of glioblastoma. We attempted to leverage the tumour specificity of our CAR in a logic gate system, however the technology was not as applicable to all binders as was suggested by the original publications.
