Developing novel CAR T cells for the treatment of glioblastoma

Background: Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell therapy uses engineered immune T cells to specifically kill cancer cells. The receptor is designed to recognise a specific target protein. CAR T cell therapies are being developed for brain cancers such as glioblastoma multiforme (GBM). GBM is the most common and aggressive malignant brain cancer and has limited treatment options. Studies have demonstrated that CAR T cells can cross the blood brain barrier and function in the brain. However, the existing CAR T cell therapies for GBM have not shown clinical benefit in patients thus far. A challenge for the field is the limited pool of validated GBM targets that is restricting the development of novel therapies. Purpose: The aim of this study is to determine whether the identification of novel GBM targets could lead to the development of an effective therapeutic.

Approach: Using cell surface proteomics data, protein targets abundantly found on primary GBM patient samples will be identified. These proteins will be assessed for use as a CAR T cell therapeutic target based on the level of protein expression i) across GBM patient samples, ii) in healthy tissue, and iii) on the cell surface, as required for CAR T cell recognition. CAR T cells will then be made against suitable targets and validated experimentally. Successful CAR T cells generated will then be tested for functional killing capacity in in vitro cell line models and in vivo mouse models.

Outcome: The potential outcome of this research could be the development and preclinical validation of novel targeted CAR T cell therapeutics for patients with glioblastoma.
