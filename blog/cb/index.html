Mitochondrial quality control activation during gram-negative bacterial infection

Background

Parkinson’s disease (PD) is a neurodegenerative disease characterised by the death of dopamine-producing neurons (DA neurons) within the substantia nigra. It has become clear that mitochondrial dysfunction is a key factor in PD pathogenesis. Agents which harm mitochondria, including bacterial pathogens, contribute to PD progression. PINK1/Parkin mitophagy and the mitochondrial unfolded protein response (UPRmt) are two major pathways that function to maintain mitochondrial health. PINK1 and Parkin are mutated in familial PD and function to maintain mitochondrial health by identifying and degrading damaged mitochondria through a selective autophagy process termed mitophagy. The UPRmt maintains mitochondrial health via an adaptive transcriptional response that repairs damaged mitochondria through the expression of chaperones and proteases. Mitochondrial dysfunction, particularly as a result of defects in PINK1 and Parkin, are known to lead to the early onset of PD. However, there is little known about the role of mitochondrial quality control and during pathogenic bacterial infection. Here activation of mitochondrial quality controls systems will be assessed after exposure to gram-negative bacterial infection in vitro, utilising a genetic model of PD. 

Hypothesis

It is hypothesised that the mitochondrial quality control pathways, PINK1/Parkin and UPRmt, act in a protective manner by limiting mitochondrial damage and reducing inflammation induced by gram-negative bacterial infection.

Approach

Primary bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) will be differentiated from the bone marrow of wild-type, Parkin-/-, CHOP-/- and Parkin-/-CHOP-/- mice. BMDMs will then be infected with gram-negative bacteria. The cytokine profile will be assessed, using flow cytometry, after the infection period. The cells will be harvested post infection and the presence or absence of key transcriptions factors that indicate activation of the UPRmt and PINK1/Parkin mitophagy will be determined.
