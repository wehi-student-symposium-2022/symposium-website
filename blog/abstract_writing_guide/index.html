A Simple Guide to Writing Your Abstract

Step 1: Plan

Consider what you want to present – you might want to focus on a particular aim or aspect of your research, or present an overview of the whole project. It can help to discuss the focus of your abstract with your supervisor and/or peers before you start writing. The format is flexible, so don’t worry if you don’t have much (or any) data to present. Start brainstorming and take inspiration from examples in the resources below!

Step 2: Write

Generally, abstracts will provide a summary of the background, methods, results and implications of your project. If you don’t have data to present, your abstract may focus more on the background, aims and future directions of the work. When writing your abstract, write in the third person (i.e. “X was used to investigate Y” rather than “we used X to investigate Y”) and keep in mind there is a 300-word limit. It can be helpful to use sub-headings to help organise your thoughts, which can be removed later or kept in as preferred.

Background: Provide some background to your project, set out the problem or question your project addresses and explain why this is important. Keep in mind your audience will be your fellow students and colleagues at WEHI. They will have different levels of expertise across different disciplines and although they will have a strong scientific background, they might not be familiar with your field.
Methods: Describe the methods you have used or will use to address the problem. This is usually a high-level overview rather than a detailed description of techniques used, unless the methodology is the main focus of the project.
Results: Summarise the results of your project. Again, this should be an overview of key findings rather than a detailed account of each result. This section isn’t applicable if you don’t have data to present – don’t be tempted to predict results you haven’t obtained yet!
Conclusions: Relate your findings to the broader field. Comment on how your findings add to the body of knowledge, and their potential applications or implications without overstating their significance.

Step 3: Check

Carefully check your grammar and spelling. It can be helpful to ask a friend or colleague to proofread as it can be difficult to spot mistakes in your own work! Send your abstract to your supervisor(s) to check – they are expert abstract writers and will be able to give valuable feedback. As you are representing their lab, it’s also important they sign off on the content and data you’re presenting.

Step 4: Submit

Once you’ve finished your abstract, fill out this short form to submit your abstract: https://forms.gle/3fvgFNMU1NwpDQ2n6

Don’t be daunted!

This symposium is for students by students, so don’t be daunted if you’ve never written an abstract before or don’t know where to begin – this is a fantastic opportunity to practise! The process of writing an abstract can really help to organise your thoughts about your project and will provide you with the opportunity to present your research in a friendly and supportive environment.
